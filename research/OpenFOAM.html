Project Overview

Research Focus: Computational modeling of tumor destruction during fast-heating radiofrequency ablation

Methods: OpenFOAM multiphysics framework with PINNs acceleration

This project focused on developing a multiphysics computational framework using OpenFOAM to model tumor destruction during fast-heating radiofrequency ablation (RFA). The objective was to predict temperature evolution, tissue damage, and treatment effectiveness while maintaining physical consistency and numerical stability.

Motivation & Context

Minimally invasive thermal therapies such as RFA are increasingly used for early-stage breast tumors due to reduced morbidity and faster recovery. Accurate prediction of temperature fields and tissue necrosis is critical for safe and effective treatment. Computational modeling enables parameter optimization and treatment planning without invasive experimentation.

Multiphysics Framework

The model couples four interacting physical processes operating across different spatial and temporal scales:

Blood Flow (Darcy-Brinkman)

Porous media flow through tumor tissue with permeability-dependent velocity fields affecting heat transfer.

Tumor Growth (Fisher Equation)

Spatiotemporal tumor cell proliferation dynamics influencing treatment planning and ablation extent.

Heat Transfer (Pennes Bioheat)

Bioheat equation with blood perfusion effects capturing convective cooling and metabolic heat generation.

Tissue Damage (Arrhenius Model)

Temperature-history dependent damage accumulation predicting extent of irreversible tissue necrosis.

Coupling Strategy: Temperature-dependent material properties and nonlinear coupling between solvers were incorporated to capture realistic ablation behavior across multiple physics domains.

OpenFOAM Implementation

Custom OpenFOAM solvers were developed to integrate porous-media flow, bioheat transfer, tumor growth, and thermal damage within a unified framework. Mesh refinement studies, timestep sensitivity analysis, and solver relaxation were used to ensure numerical stability and convergence.

Computational Challenges

Fully coupled 3D simulations were computationally expensive due to nonlinear interdependence between governing equations and large domain sizes. These challenges limited the feasibility of rapid parametric studies and real-time optimization using traditional CFD alone.

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

Hybrid Approach: Physics-Informed Neural Networks complement OpenFOAM by providing rapid surrogate predictions while maintaining physical constraints through governing equations embedded in loss functions.

To address computational limitations, Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) were explored as a complementary approach. PINNs embed governing equations directly into the loss function, enabling rapid prediction of temperature and damage fields while preserving physical constraints.

Physics Enforcement

Governing equations embedded in loss function ensure predictions satisfy physical laws without training data.

Rapid Prediction

Once trained, PINNs provide near-instantaneous predictions enabling real-time parametric exploration.

Benchmark Validation

Validated on lid-driven cavity flow showing excellent agreement with OpenFOAM reference solutions.

Surrogate Modeling

Trained surrogate models interpolate between OpenFOAM solutions for parameter ranges of interest.

Mesh-Free Approach

Neural networks operate directly on governing equations without requiring structured grids.

Gradient Information

Automatic differentiation provides exact derivatives for flux calculations and sensitivity analysis.

Validation Results: Close agreement between PINN predictions and OpenFOAM solutions demonstrated on benchmark CFD problems (lid-driven cavity flow), confirming methodology for complex multiphysics applications.
