Problem Context

In small manufacturing environments, inventory and procurement processes are often managed manually through phone calls, spreadsheets, and informal vendor coordination. This approach leads to delayed replenishment, production downtime, limited demand visibility, and heavy dependence on human intervention.

System Objectives

  • Enable real-time visibility of inventory levels
  • Reduce production interruptions caused by stock-outs
  • Support proactive and repeatable procurement decisions
  • Minimize manual vendor follow-ups
  • Introduce data-driven planning into daily operations

System Architecture

The system was designed as a centralized digital workflow integrating inventory tracking, sales logging, demand analysis, and procurement decision logic. Every transaction updates the inventory state, enabling continuous monitoring and informed decision-making.

  • Inventory database with item-level tracking
  • Sales and dispatch logging module
  • Demand trend analysis component
  • Reorder decision logic with threshold-based rules
  • Vendor database with lead-time parameters

Procurement Decision Logic

Procurement decisions were driven by a rule-based logic combining current inventory levels, historical consumption rates, safety stock requirements, and vendor lead times.

  • Dynamic reorder point calculation
  • Lead-time-aware replenishment scheduling
  • Demand trend monitoring for early risk detection
  • Safety stock buffers for critical components

Implementation & Validation

The system logic was validated using historical inventory and sales data to simulate normal and peak-demand scenarios. Stock-out conditions were modeled to evaluate system response time and reorder accuracy compared to manual procurement practices.

Performance metrics such as stock availability, response latency, and reorder reliability were monitored to assess effectiveness.

Outcomes & Impact

  • Improved visibility of inventory status across operations
  • Reduced reliance on continuous vendor communication
  • Lower probability of unexpected production stoppages
  • More structured and repeatable procurement workflows

Key Learnings

This project demonstrated how system-level thinking and decision logic can significantly improve manufacturing operations without altering physical infrastructure. It reinforced the importance of integrating data flow, operational constraints, and engineering judgment into robust system design.
